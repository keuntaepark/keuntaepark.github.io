Demand Forecasting with Advanced Time Series Models

I use a monthly demand dataset (1990-2023, 408 points) containing pre-decomposed components (Trend, Cyclical, Seasonal, Irregular) to:

  • Compare ARIMA, SARIMA, and Prophet models.
  • Provide business-relevant forecasting insights.
  • Highlight how to choose the right model for real-world decisions.

def calc_metrics(true, pred, name):
    mae = mean_absolute_error(true, pred)
    rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(true, pred))
    print(f"{name} -> MAE: {mae:.2f}, RMSE: {rmse:.2f}")

Forecasting Model Comparison Summary

We evaluated three models on 12-month holdout forecasts:

  • SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1,12)

    • MAE: 7.71
    • RMSE: 8.51
    • Captures seasonality and trend; strong baseline.

  • SARIMAX(1,1,1)(1,1,1,12) + Exogenous Drivers

    • MAE: 7.71
    • RMSE: 8.51
    • No improvement over SARIMA; simulated external variables had negligible effect.

  • Prophet

    • MAE: 22.83
    • RMSE: 23.77
    • Flexible trend + seasonality, but underperformed compared to SARIMA on this dataset.

Takeaway: For now, SARIMA/SARIMAX provides the best balance of accuracy and interpretability.

To further improve, integrate real business drivers (e.g., marketing campaigns, economic indicators) and explore ensemble or machine learning models.

Recommended Next Steps

  • Work with business teams to identify real external drivers that affect demand.
  • Integrate proxy or public datasets if direct company data is unavailable (e.g., holiday calendars, macroeconomic indicators).
  • Run feature importance analysis (e.g., random forest or correlation checks) to prioritize variables before re-feeding into SARIMAX.

These steps help transition the modeling from pure statistical fitting to actionable business forecasting.
