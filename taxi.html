Project

Analyzed 3.4M+ NYC Yellow Taxi trips to uncover how the $2.50 congestion surcharge operates in practice and to design actionable, data-backed policy improvements. The approach combined policy analysis, quantitative impact estimation, and simulation of alternative scenarios.

  • Step 1 â€“ Understanding Current Policy: Built classification models (Logistic Regression, Random Forest) to identify surcharge triggers; achieved ROC-AUC 0.91, using SHAP to uncover key drivers like trip distance, pickup zones, and time of day.
  • Step 2 â€“ Estimating Impact: Applied Ridge Regression to quantify how trip and location variables influenced surcharge amounts; this set the stage for realistic scenario testing.
  • Step 3 â€“ Testing Alternatives: Simulated zone-based and peak-hour pricing models; results showed peak-hour adjustments could boost revenue by +1.56%, while zone-based tweaks improved fairness in underserved areas.

Key Results

  • Revenue Impact: +0.9% total gain by combining time- and zone-based strategies.
  • Model Performance: Classification (ROC-AUC 0.91), Regression (lowest MAE, highest RÂ² via Ridge).
  • Policy Takeaway: Demonstrated how data-driven congestion pricing can balance operational goals with social equity.

Business Relevance

  • Delivered interpretable model outputs using SHAP, making key drivers (e.g., trip distance, zone, time) transparent to cross-functional teams.
  • Structured the simulation framework with modular design, enabling easy parameter adjustments for dynamic pricing, logistics, and urban planning applications.
  • Translated model outputs into scenario simulations showing how policy changes would affect key outcomes like revenue, fairness, and operational efficiency, providing concrete improvement recommendations.

Project Reflection

  • Project: Focused on understanding how congestion surcharges affect taxi operations and explored data-backed ways to adjust policies.
  • Problem: Managed large, inconsistent datasets and had to balance improving revenue without introducing new unfairness across time and zones.
  • Process: Cleaned missing values (e.g., fare, distance, passenger count) and removed outliers (e.g., trips >100 miles, negative fares), prioritized interpretable models, used SHAP to clarify key drivers, and ran targeted simulations to check policy impacts.
  • Outcome: Delivered clear, practical insights on where small pricing adjustments could improve both revenue and fairness. Learned to work with imperfect data while focusing on actionable, realistic outputs.
